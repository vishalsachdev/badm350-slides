# Part 1
## The Problem with Most Data

---

# This Is What Most People Do

<div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 2em; margin-top: 1em;">

<div>

### Data Dump

- 47 charts on one dashboard
- Rainbow color scheme
- No labels, no annotations
- Default Excel formatting
- "Here's the data" (end of presentation)

</div>

<div>

### Data Story

- 2–3 charts, each with a purpose
- Color highlights the insight
- Annotations explain the "so what"
- Clear narrative arc
- "Here's what you should do" (call to action)

</div>

</div>

<v-click>

<div style="margin-top: 1.5em; text-align: center; font-size: 1.3em; font-weight: bold; color: #ff5f05;">

The difference isn't the data. It's the storytelling.

</div>

</v-click>

---

# Why Does This Matter?

<div style="font-size: 1.1em; line-height: 1.8; margin-top: 1em;">

- Executives spend **~30 seconds** on a dashboard before deciding if it's useful
- The #1 complaint about analyst presentations: *"So what?"*
- The best insight in the world is worthless if nobody acts on it

</div>

<v-click>

<div style="background: #f0f0f5; border-left: 4px solid #12284c; padding: 1.5em; border-radius: 8px; margin-top: 1.5em;">

> "The goal is not to make the data beautiful. The goal is to make the data **understood**."
>
> — Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic, *Storytelling with Data*

</div>

</v-click>

# Part 2
## The Narrative Arc for Data

---

# Every Data Story Has Three Acts

<div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr 1fr; gap: 0; margin-top: 1.5em;">

<div style="background: #e8edf2; border-left: 5px solid #12284c; padding: 1.5em; border-radius: 8px 0 0 8px;">

### 1. CONTEXT
**Set the scene**

- Who is the audience?
- What do they need to know?
- Why should they care?

</div>

<div style="background: #e8edf2; border-left: 5px solid #1e3a5f; padding: 1.5em;">

### 2. CONFLICT
**Show the data**

- What does the data reveal?
- What's the problem or opportunity?
- What's at stake?

</div>

<div style="background: #fff0e6; border-left: 5px solid #ff5f05; padding: 1.5em; border-radius: 0 8px 8px 0;">

### 3. RESOLUTION
**Recommend action**

- What should we do?
- What's the expected impact?
- What's the ask?

</div>

</div>

<div style="text-align: center; margin-top: 1.5em; font-size: 1.8em; color: #ff5f05;">

→ → →

</div>

---

# Act 1: Context — Set the Scene

<div style="margin-top: 1em; font-size: 1.05em;">

Answer three questions before showing any data:

</div>

<v-clicks>

- **Who is the audience?**
  - A CEO needs the headline. An analyst needs the methodology.
  - Same data, different story.

- **What do they already know?**
  - Don't explain what's obvious. Skip to the gap.

- **Why should they care right now?**
  - Tie it to a decision they're about to make, a deadline, or a risk.

</v-clicks>

---

# Act 2: Conflict — Show the Data

<div style="margin-top: 1em; font-size: 1.05em;">

This is where your charts live. But not all your charts — just the ones that reveal:

</div>

<v-clicks>

- **The surprise** — what's unexpected or counterintuitive?
- **The trend** — what's changing, and how fast?
- **The gap** — what's the difference between where we are and where we should be?

</v-clicks>

<v-click>

<div style="background: #fff5ee; border-left: 4px solid #ff5f05; padding: 1em; border-radius: 8px; margin-top: 1.5em;">

**Pro tip:** If your chart doesn't support the story, cut it. More charts ≠ more convincing. 2–4 focused charts beat 15 unfocused ones.

</div>

</v-click>

---

# Act 3: Resolution — Recommend Action

<div style="margin-top: 1em; font-size: 1.05em;">

This is where most data presentations fail. They show data, then stop.

</div>

<v-clicks>

- **What specifically should the audience do?**
  - Not "we should improve retention" — that's obvious
  - "We should email churning users within 48 hours of their last login with a personalized offer"

- **What's the expected impact?**
  - "Based on the data, this could recover 15% of at-risk users"

- **What's the ask?**
  - "I need $5K budget and 2 weeks to run a pilot"

</v-clicks>

# Case Study
## Spotify Wrapped

---

# Why Spotify Wrapped Works

<div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 2em; margin-top: 1em;">

<div>

### What They Do

- 365 days of listening data
- Condensed into ~10 screens
- Released annually in December
- Shared 150M+ times in 2024

</div>

<div>

### Why It Works

- **Personal** — it's YOUR data
- **Shareable** — social identity signal
- **Simple** — minutes, top artists, genres
- **Emotional** — nostalgia, surprise
- **Actionable** — "discover more like this"

</div>

</div>

---

# Wrapped Uses the Narrative Arc

<div style="margin-top: 1em; font-size: 1.05em;">

| Act | What Wrapped Does |
|-----|------------------|
| **Context** | "You listened to 45,000 minutes this year" — sets the scale |
| **Conflict** | "Your top genre was... *death metal*?" — the surprise, the identity reveal |
| **Resolution** | "Here's your 2024 playlist" — gives you something to **do** |

</div>

<v-click>

<div style="margin-top: 1.5em;">

### Discussion Questions

- How does Wrapped make **365 days of data** feel manageable?
- What data do they **leave out** — and why is that a smart choice?
- How would Wrapped fail if it showed **every metric** instead of a curated story?

</div>

</v-click>

# Part 3
## Chart Makeover

---

# Principles of Chart Decluttering

<v-clicks>

- **Remove chartjunk** — 3D effects, heavy gridlines, unnecessary borders, gradient fills
- **Grey out the unimportant** — let the key data point stand out
- **Annotate directly** — put the insight ON the chart, not in a legend people won't read
- **Use color with purpose** — one highlight color for the key message, grey for context
- **Align and simplify** — left-align text, remove redundant labels, use consistent scales

</v-clicks>

<v-click>

<div style="margin-top: 1.5em; text-align: center; font-size: 1.2em; font-weight: bold;">

One chart, one message.

</div>

</v-click>

---

# Before → After: The Idea

<div style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 2em; margin-top: 1.5em;">

<div style="background: #fef0f0; border: 1px solid #e57373; padding: 1.5em; border-radius: 8px;">

### Before ❌
- Rainbow-colored bars
- 3D perspective
- Busy gridlines
- Legend at the bottom
- Title: "Q3 Revenue by Region"
- No insight, no annotation

</div>

<div style="background: #eaf5ea; border: 1px solid #4caf50; padding: 1.5em; border-radius: 8px;">

### After ✅
- Grey bars + one orange highlight
- Flat, 2D
- Minimal gridlines
- Direct labels on bars
- Title: "APAC revenue grew 34% — outpacing all other regions"
- Arrow annotation on the key bar

</div>

</div>

<v-click>

<div style="margin-top: 1em; text-align: center; font-style: italic; color: #888;">

The title IS the insight. If your title could apply to any chart, it's not specific enough.

</div>

</v-click>

# Part 4
## The "So What?" Test

---

# Three Questions for Every Chart

<div style="margin-top: 1.5em;">

For every chart, data point, or paragraph in your data story, ask:

</div>

<v-clicks>

<div style="background: #f5f5f5; border: 1px solid #ddd; padding: 1.5em; border-radius: 8px; margin-top: 1em;">

### 1. So what?
Why does this matter? What's the implication?

</div>

<div style="background: #f5f5f5; border: 1px solid #ddd; padding: 1.5em; border-radius: 8px; margin-top: 0.5em;">

### 2. Now what?
What action does this imply? What should someone do?

</div>

<div style="background: #f5f5f5; border: 1px solid #ddd; padding: 1.5em; border-radius: 8px; margin-top: 0.5em;">

### 3. What if?
What happens if we don't act? What's at stake?

</div>

</v-clicks>

<v-click>

<div style="margin-top: 1em; text-align: center; font-weight: bold; color: #d32f2f;">

If you can't answer all three → the data isn't telling a story yet.

</div>

</v-click>

---

# Applying the Test

<div style="margin-top: 1em;">

**Example:** "Our app has 200 daily active users."

</div>

<v-clicks>

| Question | Weak Answer | Strong Answer |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **So what?** | "That's our user count" | "We've grown 3x since launch but 60% drop off after day 2" |
| **Now what?** | "We should get more users" | "We should fix the onboarding flow — that's where we lose them" |
| **What if?** | "We'll have fewer users" | "At current churn, we'll be back to launch numbers in 6 weeks" |

</v-clicks>

<v-click>

<div style="margin-top: 1em; font-style: italic; color: #888;">

The strong answers turn a number into a narrative with urgency.

</div>

</v-click>

---

# 🛠️ Pair Exercise: "So What?" Challenge

<div style="font-size: 1.05em; margin-top: 1em;">

**In pairs** (8 minutes):

1. Pick a data point from your project (or make one up):
   - "We have X users"
   - "Feature Y gets Z clicks"
   - "Revenue is $X per month"

2. Partner applies the "So What?" test — pushes back until the answers are specific and actionable

3. Switch roles

</div>

<div style="background: #fff5ee; border-left: 4px solid #ff5f05; padding: 1em; border-radius: 8px; margin-top: 1em;">

**The feedback frame:**
- *"Why should I care about this number?"*
- *"What specifically should I do about it?"*
- *"What happens in 30 days if I ignore this?"*

</div>

# Key Takeaways

<div style="font-size: 1.1em; text-align: left; max-width: 600px;">

<v-clicks>

1. **Data ≠ Story.** Raw numbers don't drive action — narratives do.

2. **Context → Conflict → Resolution.** Every data story needs all three.

3. **One chart, one message.** Declutter ruthlessly.

4. **Apply the "So What?" test** to everything before you present it.

5. **The title is the insight.** If your title is a label, rewrite it.

</v-clicks>
